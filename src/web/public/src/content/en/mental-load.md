sections:
  - title: "What mental load looks like at home"
    body: >-
      One person keeps track of everything. They know when the school events are,
      what the fridge is missing, who needs to be where on Friday,
      and which appointment renewal is coming up.
      Others depend on being told what matters.
      Nobody planned it this way. It became a pattern.
    items:
      - "One person keeps track of everything."
      - "Others depend on being told what matters."
      - "Plans are split across calendars, chats, and memory."
      - "Chores are renegotiated repeatedly."
      - "Responsibility is assumed, not visible."
      - "Busy days become obvious too late."
  - title: "Why it creates friction"
    body: >-
      When the person carrying the load gets tired, overwhelmed, or simply wants
      to step back, the system breaks. Things fall through.
      Not because others are careless — but because nobody else has the full picture.
      The problem is not effort. The problem is that the system is invisible.
  - title: "Why normal tools do not solve it"
    body: >-
      A calendar shows time. A task app shows a list.
      Neither shows who is responsible.
      Neither shows what the household needs to know to keep running.
      Fragmented tools leave the coordination layer invisible.
      The mental load stays on one person because no tool models the home as a shared system.
  - title: "What changes with a shared household system"
    body: >-
      When household state becomes visible to everyone, the coordination work can be shared.
      Nothing depends on one person's memory.
      Responsibility becomes explicit. Plans, tasks, and chores appear in one place.
    items:
      - "Nothing important depends on one person's memory."
      - "Everyone can see what matters today."
      - "Responsibility becomes visible instead of implied."
      - "Chores stop becoming repeated negotiation."
      - "The household reacts less and anticipates more."
